Last June, after the twins were born, I realized that my usual "coast on former training" strategy for health wasn't going to cut it in the face of a double dose of sleep deprivation and decided to start lifting weights again. I have a squat rack in the garage, and the ability to escape for a 20 minute session at oddball hours, and actually make some measurable progress on a personal goal, felt like a good way to maintain some sanity.

I decided that I would try and put on a bunch of muscle and push my powerlifting numbers up. The only reason to do this would be so that when sympathetic friends and family asked me, "so, how are you and Jenna surviving parenting twins? Are you destroyed?" I could reply, "Actually..."

I'm nothing special, but I did manage to make some progress, and thought I'd lay out the resources that helped me get my head around lifting technique and an approach to getting strong that worked well.

**Phase One — Jocko**

First phase with Spencer, lots of Jocko workouts. Lots of bodyweight squats, plus we dug up some Stu Smith. I also dug out Dan John's essay collection: [https://amzn.to/3EYS908](https://amzn.to/3EYS908) this dude is a legendary athlete and strength coach and a great writer. I read this maybe 8 years ago, and weirdly enough it got me to start flossing regularly... but this was really helpful on re-read for thinking through what I was trying to do, why get strong, how to think about goals, etc

He mentioned in the book this Mass Made Simple program, centered around 50 rep squats. Holy mackerel. I decided around the end of June that I wanted to try and get strong, and went down the road of Dan John's "Mass Made Simple" program because the 50 rep squat set sounded like a crazy challenge. Here's an article on that program: [https://www.t-nation.com/training/mass-made-simple/](https://www.t-nation.com/training/mass-made-simple/)

I decided to go for it, kind of blowing up my workouts with Spencer. Really any structure at all seems to kill my ability to do stuff with other people.

**Phase 2 — Mass Made Simple**

This program was similar to the Super Squats program I did back after college.

Here is a crazy program I did after college that got me really strong on squats: [https://amzn.to/3JkwPoe](https://amzn.to/3JkwPoe) I drank a gallon of milk a day, literally, and worked up from a 20 rep set at 135 to 20 reps at 265 by the time I flamed out. I don't think I ever got that strong again until this past summer...

That's a great read but I wouldn't recommend doing that program unless you are really fired up to suffer.

I went from maybe  163 to 1 78, and ended up hitting 52 reps at 205, which felt pretty nuts. I can stick some videos in, no squat shoes, thought I had things pretty figured out.

**Phase 3: Easy Strength**

What to do next? I was bigger, but I wasn't terribly strong... I decided to do another Dan John program.

Next I chilled out for maybe 8 weeks on Dan John's "Easy Strength" program: [https://danjohnuniversity.com/essays/even-easier-strength](https://danjohnuniversity.com/essays/even-easier-strength) specifically this Even Easier Strength" variation. That got my deadlift up pretty high, surprisingly, but I stalled out at a 405-for-one-rep-that-destroyed-me level. Turns out max out singles like this hurt more than they help, since they torch you and kill your following few sessions.

While I was doing this I found Alan Thrall's videos on technique, for example [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UFs6E3Ti1jg](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UFs6E3Ti1jg), and started watching ALL of his stuff. Such a great resource, absorb all of this!

I like Brian Alsruhe too:  [https://www.youtube.com/channel/UC8Rt7E2JVz4fPmC8AhjDoaA](https://www.youtube.com/channel/UC8Rt7E2JVz4fPmC8AhjDoaA)

After Even Easier Strength I felt pretty intimidated by the idea of pushing the deadlift higher... but I also came around to the idea that a 500 pound deadlift would be possible with some dedicated work, and a cool milestone that my college self would never have believed I could hit.

## Phase 4: The Bridge

I decided to do the "Bridge" program by the Barbell Medicine coaches: [https://www.barbellmedicine.com/](https://www.barbellmedicine.com/)

This was a three day a week lifting program with a ton of volume, built around this idea of RPE, adjusting your weights not on some percentage of what you think your max is, but based on your perception, tuned through experience, of how many reps you have left in the tank. This was HARD with way more volume than the even easier strength program, but got my deadilft up to 440 and my squat to 370. I really want to get a bodyweight overhead press at some point... I came within a few pounds, but the overhead press was the hardest exercise to swallow the "leave a few in the tank" lesson, so still no dice here.

## Phase 5: No Plan!!

I finished this up a while back now and have been doing sporadic easy strength style workouts to maintain... well, any of the big gains. I actually lost a few pounds on the Bridge program, as I was getting much stronger.

Another twist, the bridge program is all about the three powerlifting exercises, but during the Even Easier Strength block I spent time on "fun" stuff that was different and interesting. I'm doing overhead squats now, thick bar deadlifts, kettlebell overhead press... I think this is the answer for what to do when you get pretty strong. Find different interesting movements and expand the horizons, do challenges with friends, just enjoy using your body outside of the bounds of specific sports and races. How great to have a huge collection of movement patterns and skills.

Also, go overboard in caring about very specific technique details on deadlift etc. It makes the process more interesting, and you'll get stronger by treating the lift as a skill that you're grooving in. So no deadlifting in squishy shoes, for example, because it kills  repeatability.

You can definitely develop some courage in the weight room too... the high rep squats take courage, no doubt about it.

The MOST FUN of all of this stuff was the weighted carries, because it was so weird and different for me. I also am a sucker for Dan John saying that these are a secret weapon that no one trains.
